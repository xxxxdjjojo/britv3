# Britestate Agent Dashboard & CRM Research
## User Interview Responses — Estate Agent / Sales & Lettings Leaders

**Date:** March 2026
**Format:** Simulated qualitative interviews (3 personas)
**Purpose:** Inform the design of Britestate's estate agent dashboard, CRM, and core workflows

---

## Persona Profiles

### Persona A — "Marcus"
**Role:** Branch Manager, mid-size corporate agency (3 branches, 14 negotiators)
**Experience:** 15 years in residential sales
**Current tools:** Reapit (CRM), Rightmove/Zoopla portals, Outlook, WhatsApp, Excel for reporting
**Tech comfort:** Moderate — relies on the system but isn't a power user

### Persona B — "Jade"
**Role:** Independent estate agent, sole practitioner with one VA
**Experience:** 6 years (left corporate to go independent 2 years ago)
**Current tools:** Alto (stripped-back package), Canva for marketing, iPhone for everything, Google Sheets
**Tech comfort:** High — comfortable with new tools, wants speed and simplicity

### Persona C — "Tom"
**Role:** Lettings Director, regional agency (5 offices, mixed sales & lettings)
**Experience:** 12 years in lettings, 3 in current director role
**Current tools:** Jupix, Fixflo for maintenance, Goodlord for referencing/tenancy, Excel dashboards
**Tech comfort:** Moderate-high — frustrated by tool fragmentation

---

## Section 1 — Current Dashboard Usage

### 1.1 "Describe the main dashboard or home screen you see when you log in."

**Marcus (Reapit):**
When I open Reapit first thing in the morning, I get a summary screen with a list of today's diary entries — viewings, valuations, phone calls I've diarised. Below that there's a count of new applicant registrations overnight and any new offers that have come in. There's a small section showing my branch's instruction pipeline (properties at valuation, instruction, live, under offer, exchanged, completed). Honestly, it feels quite dated — a lot of tables and lists, not much visual punch. There's also a "tasks" panel on the right-hand side showing overdue follow-ups, but it's easy to ignore because it's always got 40+ items on it and nothing is prioritised.

**Jade (Alto):**
Alto's home screen is fairly clean. I see my appointments for the day, a count of new enquiries, and my active listings with their status. There's a recent activity feed which shows the last few things that happened — new match sent, viewing confirmed, that sort of thing. It's okay for a quick snapshot, but it doesn't tell me what I should actually *do* next. I still have to click into three or four different sections to figure out my priorities. There's a little graph showing enquiries over the last 30 days but honestly I glance at it and move on.

**Tom (Jupix):**
Jupix gives me a dashboard with widgets I can sort of customise. I've got my diary, a tenancy renewals widget, arrears summary, compliance certificates expiring soon, and new maintenance requests. For lettings, the compliance piece is critical — if a gas safety certificate expires and I miss it, that's a legal issue. The problem is the dashboard tries to show me everything and ends up showing me nothing useful. The widgets are tiny, the data is cramped, and half the time I just bypass the dashboard entirely and go straight to the tenancy list or the arrears report.

### 1.2 "Which parts do you actually look at or interact with most?"

**Marcus:**
The diary, without question. That's my day — who I'm seeing, where I'm going. After that, I check new offers because they need immediate action. Then I'll look at the applicant matches to see if any hot buyers came in overnight. The pipeline view I check maybe twice a week when I'm doing my management numbers. Everything else I rarely touch on the dashboard itself.

**Jade:**
My diary and my enquiries inbox. Those two things drive everything. If I have a new enquiry, I need to respond within five minutes — that's when you win or lose the instruction. I also check my listings page constantly to see view counts and which properties are getting traction. My phone buzzes with portal notifications, and I cross-check those against Alto to make sure nothing's slipped.

**Tom:**
Compliance alerts — that's the one thing that can't wait. If I see a red flag for an expired gas cert, I'm on it immediately. After that, arrears. Which tenants are late? Who needs a chase? Then I look at the maintenance pipeline to see if any jobs are stuck. For lettings, the daily rhythm is very different from sales — it's about keeping existing tenancies running smoothly, not just chasing new business.

### 1.3 "What tabs or sections do you keep pinned or always open?"

**Marcus:**
I always have three things open: the diary (daily view), the applicant list filtered to "hot" applicants, and the property list filtered to "live" or "under offer." I also keep Outlook open alongside Reapit because so much communication happens via email, and Reapit's built-in email integration is unreliable.

**Jade:**
I keep my phone's portal apps open (Rightmove and Zoopla agent apps), Alto's enquiry inbox, and my Google Sheet where I track my pipeline manually. I know I should use Alto's pipeline, but it doesn't show me the numbers I care about — projected commission, where things are likely to fall through, and which sales are dragging.

**Tom:**
The tenancy list filtered by renewals due in the next 60 days, the maintenance request queue, and the arrears report. I've also always got Fixflo open in another tab for maintenance, and Goodlord for referencing. It's three systems minimum at all times, often four or five if you count email and WhatsApp.

### 1.4 "What information do you find yourself checking multiple times a day?"

**Marcus:**
New enquiries and viewing confirmations. These are time-sensitive — if an applicant requests a viewing at 9am and I don't confirm until 2pm, they've probably booked with another agent already. I also re-check offers multiple times because vendor conversations evolve throughout the day. And honestly, I check WhatsApp more than Reapit for vendor comms because that's where vendors actually reply.

**Jade:**
Portal stats — how many views, how many clicks, how many saves each listing has. If a property has been live for a week and the views are low, I know I need to tweak the price or the photos. I also check my phone constantly for new leads. Speed to lead is everything when you're an independent — I don't have a team to pick up the phone, so if I miss that first call, it's gone.

**Tom:**
Arrears. Full stop. I check the arrears report at 9am, 1pm, and 5pm. Missing rent is the number one issue landlords call me about, so I need to be ahead of it. I also check maintenance requests repeatedly because tenants get frustrated quickly if they don't hear back, and a frustrated tenant is a tenant who won't renew.

### 1.5 "What information is technically there but you almost never use or trust?"

**Marcus:**
The "performance analytics" section in Reapit. The data is always slightly wrong because our team doesn't log everything consistently — if someone takes a viewing phone call and forgets to log it, the conversion rates are meaningless. Also, the "time on market" stat includes all the time a property was under offer and fell through, so it inflates the numbers. I don't trust it enough to show my directors.

**Jade:**
Alto has a "marketing" section that shows social media post templates and email campaign tools. I've never used them because they look generic and I'd rather do my own branding in Canva. The CRM "notes" section is also mostly useless — it's just a free-text box with no structure, so finding anything from a previous conversation is impossible.

**Tom:**
Jupix has a "financial summary" that's supposed to show income vs. expenses per property. In theory it's brilliant, but it only works if every single transaction is logged perfectly, which it never is. Half our landlords pay us via bank transfer and we have to manually reconcile, so the figures are always out. I gave up using it 18 months ago and went back to Excel.

## Section 2 — Time Sinks and Workflows

### 2.1 "Walk me through your day from first log-in to end of day."

**Marcus:**

| Time | Activity | System(s) Used |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 8:00 | Check overnight portal enquiries, respond to urgent ones | Rightmove/Zoopla apps, Reapit |
| 8:30 | Morning team meeting — review pipeline, discuss day's viewings | Whiteboard / Reapit pipeline (projected on screen) |
| 9:00 | Return vendor calls, chase solicitors on active sales | Phone, Reapit notes, Outlook |
| 10:00–12:00 | Viewings and valuations (out of office) | Phone, Google Maps, property details printed/on phone |
| 12:30 | Log viewing feedback, send vendor updates | Reapit, Outlook, WhatsApp |
| 13:00 | Lunch + catch up on new applicants, match them to properties | Reapit applicant matching |
| 14:00–16:00 | More viewings, follow-up calls to applicants from morning viewings | Phone, Reapit |
| 16:00 | Process any offers received, negotiate, update vendors | Reapit, phone, WhatsApp |
| 17:00 | Admin — log all activities, update CRM notes, prepare tomorrow's diary | Reapit, Outlook |
| 17:30 | Quick check on progression — any exchanged? Any fall-throughs? | Reapit pipeline, phone to solicitors |

**Time loss:** The biggest time sink is between viewings — manually logging feedback, then switching to Outlook to email the vendor, then switching to WhatsApp to message the vendor because they never check email. That feedback loop takes 15 minutes per viewing when it should take 2. Also, matching applicants is painful — I search manually by criteria, scroll through lists, and half the time the matching algorithm throws up irrelevant results because the data isn't clean.

**Jade:**

My day is more fluid because I'm doing everything myself. I wake up and check my phone before I'm even out of bed — portal notifications, WhatsApp messages, emails. By 8am I've already responded to any overnight enquiries. I spend 9–12 doing viewings, valuations, or property photography. Afternoons are admin — updating listings, chasing progression, doing social media posts. Evenings are often more viewings because that's when buyers are free.

**Time loss:** I lose the most time on admin that doesn't directly generate revenue. Uploading photos to portals (Rightmove wants them one way, Zoopla slightly differently), writing property descriptions, manually sending viewing confirmations. Also, I'm constantly context-switching between my phone and my laptop. I'll take notes on my phone during a viewing, then have to re-type them into Alto when I get home. There's no good mobile workflow.

**Tom:**

| Time | Activity | System(s) Used |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 8:30 | Check arrears report, flag overdue rents | Jupix, bank statements |
| 9:00 | Chase arrears by phone and text | Phone, SMS templates |
| 9:30 | Review maintenance requests, assign contractors | Fixflo, phone, WhatsApp |
| 10:00 | Process new tenant applications, run referencing | Goodlord, Jupix |
| 11:00 | Compliance check — certificates expiring this month | Jupix compliance widget, Excel tracker |
| 12:00 | Landlord calls — property updates, rent reviews, issues | Phone, Jupix property records |
| 13:00 | Lunch |  |
| 13:30 | Process check-ins/check-outs, inventory reviews | Inventory app, Jupix |
| 14:30 | New property listings — photos, descriptions, portal upload | Jupix, portal feeds |
| 15:30 | Tenancy renewals — prepare new agreements, negotiate terms | Jupix, Goodlord, Word templates |
| 16:30 | End-of-day reporting for directors | Excel, Jupix exports |

**Time loss:** The tool-switching is a killer. I'm constantly alt-tabbing between Jupix, Fixflo, Goodlord, my email, and WhatsApp. No single system does everything I need. Maintenance is the worst — a tenant reports a leaky tap via the portal, it appears in Fixflo, I have to find a contractor (often by scrolling through my phone contacts), arrange access via WhatsApp, then update Fixflo, then update the landlord via email, then log it in Jupix. That's five systems for one leaky tap.

### 2.2 "For each key workflow, which views or pages are essential?"

**All three personas identified these core workflows:**

| Workflow | Essential Views | Current Pain |
|----------|-----------------|--------------|
| **New enquiry** | Enquiry inbox, applicant record, matching properties | Too many clicks to see if the enquiry matches existing stock |
| **Scheduling a viewing** | Diary/calendar, property details, applicant contact | Manual confirmation emails, no good two-way scheduling |
| **Sending follow-up** | Viewing feedback, applicant record, email/SMS templates | No automation — every follow-up is manual |
| **Negotiating an offer** | Offer log, vendor contact, comparable evidence | No side-by-side offer comparison, no live chain visibility |
| **Progressing a sale** | Pipeline/chain tracker, solicitor contacts, milestone checklist | Progress updates require phoning solicitors — no integration |
| **Lettings: Arrears chase** | Arrears report, tenant contact, payment history | Bank reconciliation is manual, no automated reminders that actually work |
| **Lettings: Maintenance** | Request queue, contractor database, tenant/landlord comms | Fragmented across 3+ systems |
| **Lettings: Compliance** | Certificate tracker, expiry alerts, document storage | Alerts are easy to miss, no contractor booking integration |

### 2.3 "What tasks do you still do outside the CRM?"

**Marcus:** WhatsApp (vendor/applicant comms — 60% of my communication is here now), Excel (weekly branch reports for directors, commission tracking), printed floorplans and brochures for viewings, paper feedback forms at open houses, phone notepad for quick notes during calls.

**Jade:** Google Sheets (pipeline tracker with projected commission), Canva (social media posts, property brochures), iPhone Notes (viewing notes), WhatsApp (everything), Instagram/Facebook for marketing, Google Calendar (synced with Alto but often drifts out of sync).

**Tom:** Excel (arrears analysis, compliance tracker — more reliable than Jupix), Fixflo (maintenance), Goodlord (referencing and tenancy creation), Word (tenancy agreements, Section 21 notices), WhatsApp (contractor and tenant comms), bank online portal (manual rent reconciliation).

**Why?** Universal answer: *"Because the CRM either can't do it, does it badly, or is slower than the workaround."*

### 2.4 "If you could reduce daily clicks by 50%, where would that reduction need to happen?"

**Marcus:** Viewing feedback and vendor updates. After every viewing, I'm clicking through 6–7 screens to log feedback, update the applicant record, email the vendor, and flag whether the buyer is interested. That should be one screen — "Viewing done → Feedback: interested / not interested / second viewing → Auto-notify vendor."

**Jade:** Enquiry-to-viewing booking. When a new lead comes in, I have to: open the enquiry, check the property, check my diary, propose a time, wait for confirmation, send a confirmation email, add it to the diary. That's 10+ clicks and 5 minutes. It should be: lead comes in → AI suggests available slot → buyer confirms → done.

**Tom:** Maintenance request lifecycle. From the moment a tenant reports an issue to the moment a contractor is booked and the landlord is notified, I'm touching 4 systems and making 15+ clicks. One system, one workflow, three clicks — that's what I need.

## Section 3 — What Makes a CRM Powerful (for PropTech)

### 3.1 "What separates a 'toy' CRM from a powerful one?"

**Lead capture and routing:**

**Marcus:** A powerful CRM captures leads from every source — Rightmove, Zoopla, OnTheMarket, your website, phone calls, walk-ins — and puts them all in one queue, instantly. The toy versions make you check each portal separately. Real power is when the system auto-matches the lead to your stock and assigns it to the right negotiator based on area or property type, not just round-robin. If a buyer enquires about a £1.2M detached in Beaconsfield, that should go to my senior negotiator, not the junior who started last week.

**Jade:** For me, it's speed. The CRM needs to notify me within seconds and let me respond from my phone with one tap. Most "toy" CRMs have a clunky mobile experience — slow to load, hard to navigate, tiny text. A powerful CRM for an independent agent is basically a mobile-first communication hub that happens to store data.

**Tom:** On the lettings side, a powerful CRM understands the tenancy lifecycle. A toy CRM treats every contact the same — it's just a list of names. A proper lettings CRM knows that this person is a tenant at this property, their rent is £1,200/month due on the 1st, their gas cert expires on 15th July, and they're 3 days late on this month's rent. Context is everything.

**Pipeline and deal stages:**

**Marcus:** I need a Kanban-style pipeline that shows me every property from valuation through to completion, with the expected fee attached. I want to drag a card from "under offer" to "exchanged" and have the system automatically update the solicitors, send a congratulations email to the buyer, and add the completion date to my diary. Most CRMs just show a list. I want a visual board.

**Jade:** Agreed on Kanban. But I also want to see projected income. Don't just show me "5 properties under offer" — show me "5 properties under offer = £32,000 in projected commission, at-risk: 2 (chain issues)." That's the number that matters.

**Tom:** For lettings, the pipeline is different — it's tenancy lifecycle stages: marketing → viewings → application → referencing → agreement → move-in → active tenancy → renewal or notice. But also, the management pipeline: arrears → chase → resolution, and maintenance: reported → assigned → in progress → completed → invoiced. Three parallel pipelines, ideally on one screen.

**Automation:**

**Marcus:** The automations that actually save time are: auto-matching new applicants to properties and sending them a curated shortlist, auto-sending viewing confirmation/reminder emails, auto-chasing solicitors for updates on set days (e.g., every Wednesday, send a "status update please" email to all active solicitors). What I don't want is the CRM auto-emailing my entire database with generic rubbish — that damages the brand.

**Jade:** Auto-follow-up after viewings. If I did a viewing at 2pm and haven't logged feedback by 6pm, nudge me. If a buyer viewed and showed interest but hasn't heard from me in 48 hours, auto-send a soft follow-up. Also, auto-chase for feedback from applicants who viewed — "Hi Sarah, thanks for viewing 14 Oak Lane yesterday. Would you like to book a second viewing or discuss an offer?"

**Tom:** Auto-reminders for compliance deadlines — 60 days before gas cert expires, auto-email the landlord and auto-create a task to book the gas engineer. Auto-rent reminders to tenants 3 days before rent is due. Auto-escalation if arrears hit 14 days — notify the landlord, flag for my review. These automations actually prevent problems rather than just creating noise.

**Centralised communication:**

**Marcus:** This is the holy grail that nobody has cracked. Every conversation about a property — every email, call, text, WhatsApp — should be in one timeline attached to that property and that contact. Instead, I've got bits in Reapit, bits in Outlook, bits on WhatsApp, and bits in my head. When a vendor calls and says "what did you tell the buyer last Tuesday?" I'm scrambling.

**Jade:** WhatsApp integration is non-negotiable for me. I'd say 70% of my client communication is on WhatsApp. If the CRM can't capture those conversations, it's only telling half the story. Email logging is fine but it's 2026 — most people under 50 prefer WhatsApp or text.

**Tom:** For lettings, I need tenant, landlord, and contractor communications all visible from the property record. When a tenant calls about a boiler issue, I need to see: (a) has this been reported before? (b) who's the approved contractor? (c) what did the landlord authorise last time? That context saves me 10 minutes per call.

**Reporting and analytics:**

**Marcus:** Branch-level and negotiator-level metrics: listings taken (instructions), viewings conducted, offers received, sales agreed, fall-throughs, completions, average time to sell, average fee, conversion from valuation to instruction. I need to be able to pull this for any date range and compare month-on-month. Right now I spend 2 hours every Monday morning building this in Excel from Reapit exports.

**Jade:** I care about three numbers: leads in, viewings done, fee income. Everything else is nice to have. Show me a trend line for those three things and I can tell instantly whether my business is healthy. I also want to know cost per lead — which portal is giving me the best return?

**Tom:** Occupancy rate, average void period, arrears as a percentage of rent roll, maintenance response time, compliance status by property, and landlord retention rate. These are the numbers that tell me if my lettings operation is healthy. I'd also love to see predicted income — based on current tenancies, what's my guaranteed rent roll for the next 6 months?

### 3.2 "Which automations actually save time vs. just sending spam?"

**Consensus across all three:**

**Time-saving automations (keep):**
- Viewing confirmation and reminder emails/texts (24h and 1h before)
- Post-viewing feedback requests to applicants
- Vendor update emails after each viewing day (bundled, not per-viewing)
- Compliance certificate expiry warnings (60-day, 30-day, 7-day)
- Rent due reminders to tenants
- New property match alerts to applicants (based on genuine criteria match)
- Solicitor chase emails on a set cadence during sale progression
- Task creation for overdue follow-ups

**Spam / noise (kill):**
- Generic "new properties in your area" blasts to the entire database
- "We haven't heard from you in a while" re-engagement emails to cold contacts
- Auto-generated social media posts that look robotic
- Excessive internal notifications for minor CRM updates
- Auto-response emails that say "we'll be in touch" without any personal follow-up

### 3.3 "How important are integrations? Which are non-negotiable?"

**Non-negotiable integrations (all three agreed):**
1. **Property portals** — Rightmove, Zoopla, OnTheMarket feed. If the CRM can't push listings to portals automatically, it's dead on arrival.
2. **Email** — Full bi-directional email sync (not just BCC logging).
3. **Calendar** — Google Calendar or Outlook sync for diary/viewings.
4. **Accounting** — Xero or QuickBooks integration for commission and fee tracking.

**Highly important:**
5. **WhatsApp Business API** — or at minimum, the ability to log WhatsApp conversations.
6. **E-signature** — DocuSign or HelloSign for contracts, memorandums, tenancy agreements.
7. **Referencing** — Goodlord, OpenRent, or similar for tenant checks (lettings).
8. **Telephony** — Click-to-call, call recording, and auto-logging (VoIP integration).

**Nice to have:**
9. Marketing tools (Mailchimp, social media scheduling)
10. Maintenance platforms (Fixflo, Plentific)
11. Land Registry data feeds
12. Companies House lookups

### 3.4 "What does your current system do really well that you'd be reluctant to lose?"

**Marcus:** Reapit's applicant matching — when the data is clean — is genuinely good. It understands that a buyer looking for "3-bed semi in Amersham under £600k" should also see the 3-bed semi in Chesham for £575k. The proximity and criteria matching is sophisticated. I'd also miss the audit trail — every action is logged, which is essential for compliance.

**Jade:** Alto's simplicity. It doesn't try to do everything, so what it does do, it does quickly. The interface is clean, it loads fast, and I can find what I need without digging through menus. I'd be reluctant to lose that speed in exchange for features I don't use.

**Tom:** Jupix's compliance tracking, despite its flaws, is the best I've seen in any lettings system. The certificate expiry alerts with RAG status (red/amber/green) have genuinely prevented legal issues. Also, the tenancy agreement generation — fill in the blanks, generate a PDF, done. That workflow is solid.

## Section 4 — Ideal Dashboard: Most Useful & Powerful Functions

### 4.1 "Blank canvas — what do you want above the fold in the first 5 seconds?"

**Marcus's ideal above-the-fold:**
1. **"Your 3 priorities right now"** — AI-generated, e.g., "Call Mrs. Patel (offer pending, vendor waiting since yesterday)", "Confirm 4 viewings for tomorrow", "Chase Harrison & Co solicitors (sale at 42 Elm Road stalled 9 days)"
2. **Today's diary** — a clean, scannable list of viewings and appointments with addresses, times, and applicant names
3. **Pipeline snapshot** — visual horizontal bar showing: 4 live, 3 under offer, 1 exchanging, total projected fees: £47,200
4. **Hot leads** — top 3 applicants most likely to make an offer, based on viewing activity and engagement

**Jade's ideal above-the-fold:**
1. **New enquiries counter with urgency indicator** — "3 new leads — oldest is 12 minutes ago" with a big "Respond" button
2. **Today's schedule** — simple timeline view
3. **Listing health** — traffic-light indicators for each active listing: green (performing well), amber (needs attention — low views, no viewings booked), red (stale — 21+ days, no interest)
4. **Revenue tracker** — "This month: £8,500 invoiced, £22,000 pipeline, £4,200 at risk"

**Tom's ideal above-the-fold:**
1. **Compliance alerts** — any red flags at the top, unmissable: "GAS CERT EXPIRES IN 7 DAYS: 14 Oak Lane — Book engineer NOW" with a one-click action
2. **Arrears summary** — total outstanding, number of tenants late, trend vs. last month
3. **Maintenance queue** — open requests by priority (emergency / urgent / routine) with response time indicators
4. **Tenancy renewals due** — next 30 days, with current rent vs. market rent comparison
5. **Occupancy dashboard** — portfolio-wide void rate, properties being marketed

### 4.2 "What alerts/notifications should appear vs. be suppressed?"

**Should appear (immediately, intrusively):**
- New offer received
- Offer accepted/rejected by vendor
- Sale fallen through / chain break
- Compliance certificate expired or expiring within 7 days
- Arrears over 7 days
- Emergency maintenance request
- Applicant cancellation less than 2 hours before viewing
- New high-value lead (£500k+ property enquiry or landlord with portfolio)

**Should appear (visible but not intrusive):**
- New enquiry from portal
- Viewing confirmation received
- Solicitor update on progression
- Maintenance job status change
- Tenant communication received
- New review received

**Should be suppressed (log only, show in activity feed):**
- CRM system updates (data syncs, portal feed refreshes)
- Low-priority task reminders (can batch into daily summary)
- Marketing email open/click stats
- Minor profile edits by contacts
- Auto-match notifications that don't meet strong criteria

### 4.3 "What one-click actions would you want from the dashboard?"

**All three personas contributed:**

| One-Click Action | Description |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Respond to lead | Pre-populated message with property details, send via SMS/WhatsApp/email |
| Confirm all tomorrow's viewings | Batch-send confirmation messages to all viewers for next day |
| Log viewing feedback | Quick form: interested / not interested / second viewing — auto-notifies vendor |
| Send vendor update | Pre-built summary of this week's activity on their property |
| Generate vendor report | PDF with views, enquiries, viewings, market comparison, agent commentary |
| Book a valuation | Click a lead → diary opens → select slot → confirmation sent |
| Chase solicitors | Template email to all active solicitors requesting progress update |
| Raise rent review | Auto-generate market comparison and proposed new rent for landlord |
| Bulk re-list | Relist all "withdrawn" or "let agreed" properties that are available again |
| Export pipeline to PDF | For weekly management meetings |

### 4.4 "Which metrics and KPIs do you actually care about?"

**Daily:**
- New leads received (and response time to first contact)
- Viewings conducted today
- Offers received today
- Properties needing action (stale, price reduction candidates, low engagement)
- Arrears status (lettings)
- Compliance alerts (lettings)

**Weekly:**
- Conversion rate: lead → viewing → offer → sale agreed
- New instructions (properties listed)
- Pipeline value (total projected commission from live + under offer)
- Viewings booked vs. viewings completed (no-show rate)
- Average days on market for active listings
- Negotiator league table (team performance comparison)
- Void period (lettings) — average days empty between tenancies

**Monthly:**
- Revenue: invoiced fees, pipeline forecast, year-to-date
- Fall-through rate and reasons
- Average sale time: instruction to completion
- Market share (listings vs. competitors in target postcode areas)
- Landlord retention rate (lettings)
- Cost per acquisition (what am I spending per portal lead?)
- NPS or review score trend

### 4.5 "If the dashboard could highlight 3 AI-driven 'things to do now,' what would they be?"

**Marcus:**
1. "Call Mrs. Chen — she's viewed 4 properties in 10 days and asked about mortgage affordability. She's ready to offer. Suggest: 22 Maple Drive (92% match, price just reduced)."
2. "14 Oak Lane has had 8 viewings in 3 weeks but zero offers. Market data suggests overpriced by £15k–£20k. Recommend: schedule vendor call to discuss price reduction."
3. "Chain alert: your sale at 7 Park Road depends on the buyer selling their flat in Clapham, which has been on market 63 days with no offer. Risk level: HIGH. Action: check chain status."

**Jade:**
1. "You have 2 unanswered enquiries older than 10 minutes. Responding within 5 minutes increases conversion by 78%. Call now."
2. "Your listing at 33 High Street has dropped below average views for comparable properties. Suggested actions: refresh lead photo, add video tour, consider portal boost."
3. "You have a gap in your diary tomorrow between 2–5pm. 3 applicants haven't had viewings yet and are free in that window. Suggested: batch viewings on these properties."

**Tom:**
1. "3 gas safety certificates expire within 14 days. Auto-book with preferred contractors or contact landlords for access arrangements. One-click: book all."
2. "Tenant at Flat 4, Riverside Court is 11 days in arrears (£1,450). Previous pattern: always pays by 15th. Suggested: polite reminder call (not formal letter yet)."
3. "Market rents in E14 have increased 6.2% YoY. You have 4 tenancies renewing this quarter below market rate. Estimated uplift if renegotiated: £380/month. Action: prepare rent review letters."

## Section 5 — Pain Points with Current Dashboards

### 5.1 "What frustrates you most?"

**Marcus:**
The sheer number of clicks to do anything useful. To log one viewing feedback, I click: Diary → Select Viewing → Open Applicant → Add Note → Select Note Type → Write Feedback → Save → Go to Property → Update Status → Notify Vendor. That's 10 clicks and 3 page loads for something I do 6–8 times a day. Also, the mobile experience is an afterthought — Reapit's mobile app feels like a shrunken desktop version, not a mobile-first tool. I end up using my phone for WhatsApp and my laptop for Reapit, which means I'm never fully in one place.

**Jade:**
Speed. When I'm in the car between viewings, I need to check my next appointment, get directions, and see the applicant's preferences — all in under 30 seconds. Alto on mobile takes 8 seconds to load the dashboard, then another 5 seconds to load the diary, then I have to scroll to find the right entry. By that time I'm already at the property. Also: no offline mode. If I'm in a rural area with poor signal (which happens surprisingly often when showing country properties), I can't access anything.

**Tom:**
Fragmentation. I fundamentally should not need 4+ software products to manage a letting. Jupix doesn't do maintenance well, so I need Fixflo. Jupix doesn't do referencing well, so I need Goodlord. Jupix doesn't do accounting well, so I need Xero. And none of them talk to each other properly — I'm the human API, manually copying data between systems. That's not a CRM, that's a filing cabinet.

### 5.2 "Where does the system push unwanted data, and where does it hide critical info?"

**Pushed but unwanted:**
- Auto-generated "insights" that state the obvious ("You had 12 viewings this week" — yes, I know, I was at all of them)
- System update notifications ("Portal feed synced successfully" — who cares?)
- Marketing campaign metrics for campaigns nobody ran
- "Suggested contacts to reconnect with" based on simple time-since-last-contact rules (these are almost always irrelevant)

**Hidden but critical:**
- Chain information — who else is in the chain, where are they in their process? This is buried or nonexistent
- Solicitor contact details and last update — always requires drilling into the transaction record
- Landlord instruction documents (management agreement, fee schedule) — buried in a document library
- Historical pricing changes — how many reductions has this property had, and when?
- Comparable evidence — recent sold prices in the immediate area, not just the postcode

**Tom:** The "social media" widget — gone. The "new feature announcements" widget — gone. The "quick search" widget — I use the main search bar, not a tiny embedded one. Essentially, anything that isn't directly about tenants, landlords, properties, or money gets hidden.

### 5.4 "If you could remove 50% of your dashboard to make the rest better?"

**Marcus would remove:** Marketing tools, news/announcements, generic reports, the quick links panel, the leaderboard in its current form, the "recent activity" feed (too noisy), and the analytics that require clean data I don't have. **Keep:** diary, pipeline, offers, hot leads, tasks.

### Key Themes for Britestate Agent Dashboard Design

1. **Speed over features.** Agents value a fast, clean interface over a feature-rich but slow one. Every additional second of load time is a second they lose to a competitor.

2. **Mobile-first is not optional.** The majority of an agent's day happens away from their desk. The dashboard must be designed for phones first, desktops second.

3. **Reduce clicks, not just add screens.** The most impactful improvements compress multi-step workflows into single actions. The "viewing feedback → vendor update" flow is the clearest opportunity.

4. **WhatsApp is the elephant in the room.** It's where 60–70% of communication happens. Any CRM that ignores WhatsApp is building on incomplete data. Integration (even if it's just logging) is a major differentiator.

5. **AI should be a copilot, not a pilot.** Agents want AI to surface priorities, suggest actions, and draft content — but they want to stay in control. The "3 things to do now" concept resonated across all personas.

6. **Lettings and sales need different dashboards.** The daily rhythm, KPIs, and workflows are fundamentally different. One-size-fits-all dashboards alienate both. Britestate's role-specific dashboard approach is exactly right.

7. **Compliance is a liability reducer, not just a feature.** For lettings, compliance tracking isn't a nice-to-have — it's the difference between a successful operation and a lawsuit. This feature should be treated with the same priority as payment processing.

8. **The single-tool promise.** Every agent interviewed expressed frustration with tool fragmentation. The agent who can work from one system — CRM, portal feeds, communication, accounting, compliance — will never leave. This is Britestate's biggest opportunity.
